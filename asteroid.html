ASTEROIDS

What are Asteroids?

Asteroids are rocky bodies that orbit the Sun, primarily found in the asteroid belt between Mars and Jupiter. They are remnants from the early solar system, composed of materials that never formed into a planet. Asteroids vary in size from tiny pebbles to about 600 miles in diameter. They can have irregular shapes and may contain metal-rich or carbon-rich compositions. Studying asteroids helps scientists understand the conditions and processes of the early solar system.

Formation and Classification

Asteroids formed from the leftover building blocks of the solar system that never coalesced into planets. They are classified based on their composition: C-type (carbonaceous), S-type (silicaceous), and M-type (metallic). C-type asteroids are the most common, making up about 75% of known asteroids. They are dark and rich in carbon. S-type asteroids are made of silicate materials and nickel-iron, while M-type asteroids are composed mainly of metallic iron-nickel.

Asteroid Impacts and Threats

Asteroids have impacted Earth throughout its history, shaping its surface and potentially influencing the evolution of life. The most famous impact is believed to have caused the extinction of the dinosaurs 65 million years ago. Today, scientists monitor Near-Earth Objects (NEOs) to identify potential threats. NASA's Planetary Defense Coordination Office is tasked with detecting, tracking, and characterizing potentially hazardous asteroids and developing strategies to mitigate the threat of an impact.
