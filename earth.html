How did Earth form?

Earth's structure is composed of four main layers: the crust, the mantle, the outer core, and the inner core. The crust is the outermost layer, consisting of solid rock and divided into continental and oceanic types. Beneath the crust lies the mantle, composed of silicate minerals rich in iron and magnesium. The outer core is a layer of molten iron and nickel, while the inner core is solid and primarily composed of iron and nickel. Studying Earth's structure helps scientists understand geological processes and the planet's history.

How does Earth fit into the Milky Way galaxy?

Earth is located in the Milky Way galaxy, which is a barred spiral galaxy containing about 100 to 400 billion stars. Specifically, Earth resides in the Orion Arm, a minor spiral arm situated about 27,000 light-years from the galactic center. The solar system orbits the Milky Way's center approximately every 225-250 million years. Understanding Earth's place in the Milky Way helps scientists study galactic structure, stellar evolution, and the potential for other habitable planets in our galaxy.

What is Earth's role in the solar system?

Earth is the third planet from the Sun and the only known planet to support life. It orbits the Sun at an average distance of about 93 million miles (150 million kilometers), within the habitable zone where conditions are suitable for liquid water. Earth's atmosphere and magnetic field protect it from harmful solar and cosmic radiation. Its position and characteristics make it a unique laboratory for studying planetary formation, climate, and the potential for life elsewhere in the solar system.
