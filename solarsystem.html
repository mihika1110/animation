SOLAR SYSTEM

What is the Solar System?

The solar system consists of the Sun, eight planets, their moons, and a variety of smaller objects such as dwarf planets, asteroids, and comets. The Sun, a medium-sized star, lies at the center of the solar system, providing the gravitational pull that keeps all these objects in orbit. The planets range from the rocky terrestrial planets like Earth to the gas giants like Jupiter. The study of our solar system helps us understand the formation and evolution of planetary systems.

Components of the Solar System

The solar system is composed of various celestial objects, including planets, moons, asteroids, comets, and dwarf planets. The terrestrial planets (Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars) are composed mostly of rock and metal. The gas giants (Jupiter and Saturn) are massive planets with thick atmospheres of hydrogen and helium. The ice giants (Uranus and Neptune) have similar compositions but also contain water, ammonia, and methane ices. Additionally, the Kuiper Belt and Oort Cloud are regions filled with icy bodies and comets.

Formation of the Solar System

The solar system formed about 4.6 billion years ago from a giant molecular cloud. A shockwave from a nearby supernova likely triggered the collapse of the cloud, leading to the formation of the Sun at its center. The remaining material flattened into a rotating disk, where dust and gas coalesced to form the planets, moons, and other objects. Studying the solar system's formation provides insights into the processes that create planetary systems throughout the galaxy.
